# ADR-022: Account identity and stakeholder reference

**Date:** 2026-05-03
**Status:** Accepted

## Context

The Account bounded context needs an identity model that supports several
non-negotiable requirements simultaneously. Accounts must be globally and
unambiguously identifiable across every other context that refers to them — the
Ledger, Account Servicing, Payments, Reporting, Reconciliation. The party
responsible for each account — the stakeholder — must be referenceable without
Nucleus holding personal data about that party, since the CLAUDE.md instruction is
unambiguous that Nucleus must not contain PII. The model must accommodate
legitimate upstream changes to a customer's identifier (mergers, restructuring,
deduplication of customer records, sole-trader-to-LLP transitions) without forcing
the cumbersome close-and-reopen workaround that an immutable identifier would
demand. And it must permit stakeholder-level views — Single Customer View
enumeration, regulatory cohort reporting, aggregate balance — without elevating the
stakeholder to a domain entity inside Nucleus.

The configurer personas (Sasha, Liam, Maya) confirm that customer identity is owned
by an upstream customer management system and that Nucleus receives only opaque
references. The reporting and reconciliation personas (Robin, Alex) require
stakeholder-level financial aggregates that are correct, performant, and auditable.
The Casey persona makes clear that the stakeholder identifier may be associated
with vulnerable individuals and that information about them must be held with care
— another reason to hold as little of it as possible.

## Decision

The Account aggregate's identifier is a UUID generated by Nucleus at the moment of
account opening. The client of the opening operation does not supply the identifier
and cannot influence its generation. Once assigned, the identifier is immutable; it
persists through every state change the account undergoes and is the basis on which
every other context refers to the account.

The stakeholder identifier is an opaque string supplied by the client at opening.
It is treated as a value, not as a key into a Nucleus-resident Stakeholder
aggregate; no Stakeholder aggregate exists in the Account context. The stakeholder
identifier is mutable while the account is `OPEN`: a configurer or operator may
update it to reflect upstream identifier changes. Updates on `PENDING_CLOSURE` or
`CLOSED` accounts are rejected, consistent with the general principle that closure
intent freezes account attributes. A successful update emits
`AccountStakeholderChanged` carrying the prior and new identifiers, the change
timestamp, and the configurer attribution. Submissions whose new identifier equals
the current value are no-ops and emit no event.

A stakeholder-identifier change does not retroactively reattribute prior events or
ledger entries. Those carry the stakeholder identifier in force at the time of
their emission and remain authoritative as the historical record. Subsequent
stakeholder-account queries reflect the post-change state.

Stakeholder-level views are produced without a Stakeholder aggregate. The set of
accounts associated with a stakeholder (Single Customer View enumeration,
regulatory cohort reporting) and the stakeholder's financial aggregates across
those accounts (total balance, aggregate P&L position) are both derived from the
account population rather than addressed against a stakeholder-keyed aggregate.

The strong recommendation, on performance, audit, and reconciliation grounds, is
that aggregate views are continuously materialised rather than computed on
demand. Stakeholder-level financial aggregates are anticipated to be held as
positions on per-stakeholder aggregation accounts maintained continuously by the
internal accounting feature, and read as ordinary balance queries against those
accounts. Set-membership views may similarly benefit from materialised indexing
maintained alongside those aggregation accounts rather than scanned on demand.
The specific mechanism by which each view is produced — query at request time,
continuous materialisation via the accounting feature, or a hybrid — is an
implementation matter that this ADR does not prescribe in detail. What it does
record is that on-demand computation is inadvisable wherever materialisation can
replace it: financial aggregates do not meet the threshold for on-demand
computation and must be materialised; set-membership views are presumed to
benefit from the same treatment unless implementation needs disclose otherwise.

## Consequences

**Positive:** The identity model respects the PII boundary. Nucleus holds no
information about who a stakeholder is, only which accounts the stakeholder owns
and the opaque reference the upstream system uses. The mutability of the
stakeholder identifier handles legitimate upstream changes without the cumbersome
close-and-reopen pattern an immutable identifier would force. The absence of a
Stakeholder aggregate avoids duplicating an upstream-owned lifecycle and resists
the temptation to accumulate stakeholder attributes that should not live in
Nucleus. The expected materialisation of aggregate views — both financial
aggregates and set-membership — via the internal accounting feature provides
performant, auditable, and reconcilable answers to stakeholder-level questions
without forcing on-demand computation.

**Negative:** Consumers requiring stakeholder-level views address them through
the Account context's query API rather than against a Stakeholder entity
directly. The implementation backing each view (continuous materialisation,
on-demand computation, hybrid indexing) is opaque to consumers and may evolve
without a contract change, but the consumer surface is uniform across mechanisms.
The stakeholder identifier's mutability requires audit-trail discipline: the
event stream is the authoritative record of which stakeholder owned an account
at which time, and downstream consumers that assume the value's immutability
would silently misattribute account ownership over time.

**Risks:** Mutating a stakeholder identifier is operationally significant. An
erroneous update on a high-value account would be detectable but not trivially
reversible — a corrective update is itself an operation, not a rollback. The risk
is mitigated by the configurer attribution recorded on every change and by the
closure-time freeze of the attribute. A more material risk is downstream consumers
that assume immutability without checking the event stream; this is mitigated by
the explicit publication of `AccountStakeholderChanged` and the documentation of
the mutability contract here.

## Alternatives Considered

A Stakeholder aggregate inside the Account context was considered. It was rejected
on three grounds. It would duplicate the customer-management lifecycle that lives
in the upstream system, creating two sources of truth that would inevitably drift.
It would have no domain content of substance once PII is excluded, leaving only an
identifier that is already carried as a value on each account. And the views that
might motivate considering one — set membership, financial aggregates — are
addressed without it.

An immutable stakeholder identifier was considered. It was rejected because it
would conflict with the opaque-tag premise: if the identifier carries no semantics,
Nucleus has no basis on which to judge whether two identifiers refer to "the same"
or "different" parties, and forcing close-and-reopen on identifier change would
import that judgement upstream into Nucleus. The cost of the cumbersome workflow
falls on configurers who have no domain reason to absorb it.

On-demand computation of stakeholder-level aggregate views — financial aggregates
in particular, but set-membership views also — by query at request time was
considered. It was rejected as the default model on performance grounds (summing
or scanning across portfolios scales poorly), audit grounds (a query-time
projection has no posted record and disappears once the response is returned),
and reconciliation grounds (Alex reconciles materialised positions against the
general ledger and requires Nucleus to hold them as posted positions, not as
query-time projections). The mechanism is not categorically forbidden — the ADR
leaves room for implementation choice per view — but the default direction is
materialisation, with on-demand computation justified case by case rather than
assumed.
